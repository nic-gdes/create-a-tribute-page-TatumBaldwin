Paula Scher Tribute

Welcome to the world of Paula Scher, a visionary graphic designer who has left an indelible mark on the global creative landscape. With a knack for seamlessly weaving together the familiar and the artistic, Scher's iconic work effortlessly bridges the realms of pop culture and fine art.

As a partner at Pentagram's New York office since 1991, Scher's influence has been felt for decades. Her pioneering approach to typography in the 1970s and 80s laid the foundation for her groundbreaking career. Notably, her transformational identity for The Public Theater in the mid-1990s redefined the visual language of cultural institutions.

More recently, her collaborative architectural endeavors have breathed new life into urban spaces, infusing them with dynamic dimensional graphic design. From Citibank to Tiffany & Co., Scher's graphic identities have become blueprints for revitalizing American brands. With an impressive portfolio encompassing diverse clients like Bloomberg, Microsoft, and the Museum of Modern Art, Scher continues to push boundaries and inspire. Explore the legacy of Paula Scher as her work graces the collections of prestigious institutions worldwide and inspires designers and artists alike.

Maps

"MAPS" presents an impressive collection of 39 artworks, including paintings, drawings, prints, and installations by Paula Scher. Notably, it features her Queens Metropolitan Campus commission and includes sizable original paintings, some towering up to 12 feet. The book captures the intricate details of these works, showcasing hand-painted place names, information, and cultural commentary. The jacket of the book unfolds into a poster depicting a portion of her 2010 painting "World Trade." The book begins with Scher's essay, "All Maps Lie," where she reflects on her father's influence as a photogrammetric engineer and his lessons about the inherent inaccuracies of maps. Her father's invention, Stereo Templates, corrected distortions in aerial photography for printed maps. Simon Winchester, author of "The Map That Changed the World," contributes a foreword, discussing the enduring allure of maps in the age of GPS

"Make it Bigger"

"Make It Bigger" by Paula Scher (2002) offers a concise overview of the designer's 30 years of work and her insights into the intricate interpersonal dynamics driving the design process. The book includes 200+ work examples, new essays, and witty diagrams depicting meeting mechanics and office layouts. Designed as a compact object, it uses Akzidenz-Grotesk typeface for readability and impact. The book's modest size contrasts with its title, reflecting a touch of absurdity.

"Works"

Paula Scher: "Works" is a comprehensive visual compilation of the influential graphic designer's career, published by Unit Editions. Spanning 522 pages, it showcases over 300 projects that trace Scher's journey from her early days in the music industry to her partnership with Pentagram. Edited by Tony Brook and Adrian Shaughnessy, the book is organized chronologically and thematically. It begins with an interview, highlights her record covers, identity design, and environmental graphics, including iconic projects for MoMA, Charter Schools, and the High Line. The book delves into her logos for global corporations, politically-motivated posters, New York Times Op-Ed illustrations, and campaigns. It also explores her distinctive hand-painted maps and her enduring collaboration with The Public Theater spanning two decades. Designed by Spin with creative direction by Brook, the book is available in softcover and a boxed edition with a signed print.

Illustration

In 1973, Paula Scher wrote her only children's book, 'The Brownstone.' Reissued by Princeton Architectural Press, it humorously depicts diverse animal neighbors in a New York City brownstone. Through playful illustrations by Stan Mack, the story teaches harmony and adaptation. The book's unique design illustrates apartment shifts like a puzzle. Originally a hit, its enduring charm led to the reissue. Scher's singular foray into children's literature stands apart from her design legacy.
